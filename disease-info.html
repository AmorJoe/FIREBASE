Skin Conditions Library

Comprehensive guide to dermatological conditions supported by our AI model.

Eczema
Inflammatory

Eczema is a condition where patches of skin become inflamed, itchy, cracked, and rough. It's often triggered by a combination of genetic factors, an overactive immune system, and environmental irritants or allergens like soaps, detergents, certain fabrics, or pet dander. Stress, hormonal changes, and extreme temperatures can also be triggers. It's not contagious but can cause significant discomfort and impact quality of life.

Melanoma
Malignant

Melanoma is the most aggressive and dangerous form of skin cancer, originating in the pigment-producing cells called melanocytes. It frequently develops from an existing mole or as a new dark, irregular spot. Intense, intermittent exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation, often from sunburns or tanning beds, is a primary cause. If not detected and treated early, melanoma can rapidly spread to other parts of the body, making early identification crucial.

Atopic Dermatitis
Chronic

Atopic Dermatitis is a chronic, inflammatory skin condition, often referred to as a common type of eczema. It typically causes intensely itchy, red, dry, and scaly patches of skin. People develop it due to a combination of genetic factors, an impaired skin barrier, and an overactive immune system. Triggers like allergens (e.g., pollen, dust mites), irritants (e.g., harsh soaps), stress, and climate changes can cause flare-ups.

Basal Cell Carcinoma
Skin Cancer

Basal Cell Carcinoma is the most prevalent form of skin cancer, arising from the basal cells in the outermost layer of the skin. It usually appears as a pearly or waxy bump, a persistent sore, or a reddish patch, typically on sun-exposed areas like the face and neck. Prolonged exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun or tanning beds is the main cause. BCC grows slowly and rarely metastasizes but can be locally destructive if left untreated.

Melanocytic Nevi
Benign

Melanocytic Nevi, commonly known as moles, are benign (non-cancerous) growths on the skin composed of melanocytes, the cells that produce pigment. They can be present at birth or develop later in life. While most moles are harmless, their formation can be influenced by genetics and sun exposure. Monitor moles for changes in size, shape, color, or texture, as these could indicate a potential transformation into melanoma.

Keratosis-like Lesions
Benign

Benign Keratosis-like Lesions encompass several non-cancerous skin growths, including seborrheic keratoses and stucco keratoses. They often appear as waxy, "stuck-on" looking spots that can be tan, brown, or black. These lesions are very common, especially as people age, and are thought to be influenced by genetics and sun exposure. They are generally harmless but can be removed if they become irritated or for cosmetic reasons.

Psoriasis & Lichen Planus
Autoimmune

Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition where skin cells multiply too quickly, leading to thick, red, scaly patches, often accompanied by itching or burning. Lichen Planus is another inflammatory condition causing purplish, itchy, flat-topped bumps on the skin or in the mouth. Both conditions are non-contagious and are believed to be caused by an overactive immune system, often triggered by stress, infections, or certain medications.

Seborrheic Keratoses
Benign Growth

Seborrheic Keratoses are extremely common, harmless skin growths that often appear as brown, black, or light tan "pasted-on" spots, increasing in number with age. Other benign skin tumors include skin tags (small, soft growths) or cherry angiomas (small, red spots). These are generally genetic and often appear spontaneously. They pose no health risk but can sometimes become irritated by clothing or friction.

Tinea Ringworm & Candidiasis
Fungal

These are common skin infections caused by various types of fungi. Tinea, or ringworm, typically presents as a red, itchy, circular rash. Candidiasis, a yeast infection, often appears in warm, moist areas of the body. People contract these infections through direct contact with infected individuals, animals, or contaminated surfaces. Factors like humidity, poor hygiene, a weakened immune system, or certain medications can lead to fungal overgrowth.

Learn more
Warts Molluscum & Viral
Viral

These are skin growths caused by various viruses. Warts, often due to HPV, are rough, firm bumps, while Molluscum Contagiosum presents as small, flesh-colored, pearly lesions. They are typically acquired through direct skin-to-skin contact, self-inoculation, or shared objects. Children and individuals with compromised immune systems are particularly susceptible. They can be itchy, irritating, or a cosmetic concern, and are contagious.
